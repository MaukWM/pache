Assess a Japanese sentence on these axes. The learner aims for NATIVE-LIKE output, so naturalness
matters as much as correctness.

- GRAMMAR — particles, conjugation, transitivity pairs (下がる/下げる), negation, and word order must
  be well-formed.
- NATURALNESS — a native would actually phrase it this way. Unnatural collocations (e.g. 対策の高さ),
  stiff/foreign phrasing, or leftover English words are NOT natural. Guard: count it unnatural only
  when a native would find it odd/foreign/wrong — NOT merely because a more elegant phrasing exists.
  Do not nitpick acceptable output.
- MEANING — accurately conveys the English sentence's meaning; nothing added or dropped.
- POLITENESS — overall register. The sentence-final predicate is the primary signal:
  - polite = 丁寧語 (です/ます)
  - casual = plain (だ / plain forms / んだ / ね / よ)
  - mixed  = a genuine mix (e.g. a casual quote inside a polite sentence)
  But also weigh vocabulary and keigo consistency (尊敬語/謙譲語, rough words, particles) — e.g. a
  rude 「お前」 clashes with a です ending. Subordinate and relative clauses normally use plain form
  even in polite speech — that is NOT mixed.
